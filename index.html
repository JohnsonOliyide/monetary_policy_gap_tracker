Monetary Policy Gap Tracker

Tracking whether monetary policy is restrictive or accommodative across alternative natural rate of interest estimates.

This dashboard compares the real policy rate with alternative estimates of the natural rate of interest, r*, to assess whether monetary policy appears restrictive, neutral, or accommodative under the selected policy rate and inflation-expectations measure.

Policy gap = (Policy rate − Expected inflation) − r*

Positive policy gaps indicate restrictive policy. Negative policy gaps indicate accommodative policy. Values close to zero suggest a neutral or ambiguous stance.

    Methodology

    The Monetary Policy Gap Tracker evaluates the stance of monetary policy by comparing the real policy rate with alternative estimates of the natural rate of interest, r*. The natural rate of interest is commonly interpreted as the real interest rate that is consistent with the economy operating near its potential and inflation remaining stable. Because r* is not directly observed, different models and market-based approaches can produce different estimates.

    The dashboard does not estimate a new r* series and does not combine existing measures into a single index. Instead, it asks a more transparent question: given a selected policy rate and a selected measure of expected inflation, does monetary policy appear restrictive or accommodative across alternative natural-rate estimates?

    Policy gapj,t = (it − πet) − r*j,t

    Here, it is the selected nominal policy rate, πet is the selected measure of expected inflation, and r*j,t is natural-rate estimate j. A positive policy gap means the real policy rate is above the selected estimate of r*, suggesting a restrictive policy stance. A negative policy gap means the real policy rate is below r*, suggesting an accommodative stance. Values close to zero suggest that policy is near neutral under that particular set of assumptions.

    Policy rate

    The default policy-rate measure is the Effective Federal Funds Rate. This is the main short-term interest rate associated with the Federal Reserve’s policy stance and is the most natural benchmark for comparing the real policy rate with estimates of the neutral real short-term rate. The dashboard also includes the 1-Year Treasury Yield as an alternative policy-rate measure. The 1-Year Treasury Yield provides a market-based rate at a one-year horizon, but it reflects expected future policy rates and other market factors, so it should be interpreted as a sensitivity check rather than the baseline policy instrument.

    Expected inflation

    To convert the nominal policy rate into a real policy rate, the dashboard subtracts a selected measure of expected inflation:

    Real policy ratet = it − πet

    Expected inflation is important because the same nominal interest rate can imply different real policy stances depending on the inflation expectation used. For example, a 5 percent nominal policy rate is more restrictive if expected inflation is 2 percent than if expected inflation is 4 percent. The dashboard includes two broad types of expected-inflation inputs: moving-average proxies and survey/model-based expectations.

    Moving-average inflation-expectation proxies

    The default expected-inflation measure is the Core PCE moving-average proxy. This proxy uses recent realized inflation as a simple measure of expected inflation. The idea is that expectations may be partly adaptive: when recent inflation has been high, the inflation rate used to form the real policy rate may also be higher. This approach is useful because it is transparent, reproducible, available over a long history, and closely related to the real-rate construction used in several natural-rate models.

    For monthly calculations, the moving-average proxy is calculated as the average of the previous 12 months of annualized month-over-month inflation:

    πe,kt = (1 / 12) × Σj=112 πkt−j, MoM annualized

    The current month is excluded, so the expectation proxy for month t uses inflation information available through month t−1. For quarterly calculations, the proxy is calculated as the average of the previous four quarters of annualized quarter-over-quarter inflation:

    πe,kq = (1 / 4) × Σj=14 πkq−j, QoQ annualized

    The dashboard includes moving-average proxies based on core PCE inflation, headline PCE inflation, core CPI inflation, and headline CPI inflation. Core PCE is the default because it is less volatile than headline inflation and is closely related to the inflation concept commonly emphasized in Federal Reserve analysis. The other options allow users to examine whether the implied stance of policy changes when expectations are proxied using broader or CPI-based inflation measures.

    Survey and model-based inflation expectations

    The dashboard also includes direct or model-based measures of inflation expectations. These measures differ from the moving-average proxies because they are not constructed mechanically from recent realized inflation. Instead, they come from surveys or models designed to measure expected inflation more directly.

    The options include Cleveland Fed 1-Year Expected Inflation, University of Michigan 1-Year Inflation Expectation, New York Fed Survey of Consumer Expectations 1-Year Inflation Expectation, and Survey of Professional Forecasters 1-Year Inflation Forecast. When the SPF measure is selected, policy-gap calculations are reported at quarterly frequency because SPF is a quarterly series. The dashboard does not manufacture monthly SPF-based policy gaps.

    Natural rate of interest measures

    The dashboard compares the real policy rate with several natural rate of interest measures. These include model-based, market-based, policymaker-implied, and simple benchmark measures. The fixed 2 percent benchmark is not an estimated natural rate. It is included as a simple reference assumption, r* = 2%, so users can compare model-based and market-based estimates with a constant real neutral-rate benchmark.

    For Laubach-Williams, the dashboard allows users to choose between the one-sided and two-sided estimate when those source series are available. The one-sided estimate uses only information available up to that date, making it more appropriate for real-time policy assessment. The two-sided estimate uses information from the full sample and is more appropriate for revised historical analysis. Holston-Laubach-Williams is treated as a current published estimate.

    Frequency treatment

    The dashboard preserves the natural frequency of each series. Monthly natural-rate measures remain monthly, while quarterly natural-rate measures remain quarterly. The dashboard does not interpolate quarterly r* estimates into monthly values.

    When a monthly inflation-expectation measure is selected, monthly natural-rate measures produce monthly policy gaps and quarterly natural-rate measures produce quarterly policy gaps. For quarterly natural-rate measures, the real policy rate is calculated at quarterly frequency. When a quarterly inflation-expectation measure, such as SPF, is selected, all policy-gap calculations are reported at quarterly frequency. Monthly natural-rate measures are converted to quarterly frequency using quarterly averages for that view.

    Interpretation

    The dashboard should be interpreted as a comparison tool rather than a single estimate of the “true” stance of policy. If most natural-rate measures imply a positive policy gap, then policy appears restrictive under the selected assumptions. If most imply a negative gap, then policy appears accommodative. If the measures disagree, then the assessment of policy stance is sensitive to the choice of r*, policy rate, or inflation-expectation measure.
